HHNewton is a high-performance computing software for nonlinear two-phase flow in porous media.
The target applications are pressure-driven subsurface processes such as supercritical CO$_2$ injection, plume migration, and water displacement in heterogeneous formations.

The project is deliberately developed along two synchronized lines:

- a **MATLAB implementation**, used for rapid prototyping, algebraic verification, and controlled numerical experiments;
- a **MOOSE/HPC implementation**, designed for distributed-memory simulations, large meshes, and production-oriented solver development.

This two-code strategy is useful in practice.
The MATLAB version makes it possible to inspect every algebraic block, test nonlinear damping strategies, and compare discretizations in a transparent environment.
The MOOSE version then transfers the verified model to an HPC stack based on libMesh, PETSc, MUMPS, and MPI-parallel sparse algebra.

## Two-phase flow model

In the present reduced setting, temperature is constant, mechanics is frozen, and dissolved CO$_2$ is neglected.
The unknowns are the liquid pressure $p_w$ and the CO$_2$ pressure $p_c$.
The capillary pressure

$$
s = p_c - p_w
$$

controls the saturation law $S_w = S_w(s)$ and therefore the nonlinear phase mobilities.
The mass balances are

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(n S_w \rho_w\right)
+ \nabla \cdot \left(\rho_w \mathbf{q}_w\right)
= Q_w,
$$

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(n (1-S_w) \rho_c\right)
+ \nabla \cdot \left(\rho_c \mathbf{q}_c\right)
= Q_c.
$$

The phase fluxes are written in Darcy form,

$$
\mathbf{q}_{\alpha}
=
-\mathbf{k}\,
\frac{k_{r\alpha}(S_w)}{\mu_{\alpha}}
\left(\nabla p_{\alpha} - \rho_{\alpha}\mathbf{g}\right),
\qquad
\alpha \in \{w,c\}.
$$

The nonlinearities enter through $S_w(s)$, the relative permeabilities $k_{rw}$ and $k_{rc}$, and the pressure-dependent densities.
This is already enough to create a stiff nonlinear problem: the injected CO$_2$ front is controlled by a strong coupling between pressure, saturation, density, and mobility.

## MATLAB reference solver

The MATLAB implementation is not only a visualization or post-processing tool.
It is a compact reference solver for the fully coupled nonlinear algebraic problem.
It keeps the same unknown structure as the HPC code,

$$
\mathbf{x}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{p}_w \\
\mathbf{p}_c
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and assembles the same residual blocks for water and CO$_2$.
This makes it possible to verify, term by term, the time derivatives, diffusion terms, saturation derivatives, and off-diagonal couplings.

One important outcome of this reference implementation is the role of coefficient placement.
For strongly nonlinear mobilities, evaluating coefficients at quadrature points or at cell averages can produce different front speeds, even with the same mesh and time step.
The MATLAB solver provides a clean finite-volume-like reference based on cell-centered coefficients.

<img src="/assets/images/07hhnewton/hhnewton_discretization_effect.svg" width="720"/>

This prototype layer reduces risk before moving to large runs:
if a nonlinear effect appears in the HPC simulation, it can be isolated and reproduced in MATLAB with a smaller algebraic system.

## MOOSE/HPC implementation

The MOOSE implementation is the production-oriented branch of HHNewton.
It assembles the residual and Jacobian directly inside the MOOSE material/kernel system and relies on the MOOSE/libMesh/PETSc stack for parallel execution.

At each Newton iteration the monolithic system has the block form

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
J_{ww} & J_{wc} \\
J_{cw} & J_{cc}
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
\delta p_w \\
\delta p_c
\end{bmatrix}
=
-
\begin{bmatrix}
R_w \\
R_c
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The diagonal blocks contain storage and phase diffusion.
The off-diagonal blocks contain the saturation coupling induced by $s=p_c-p_w$.
The material layer provides not only the physical properties, but also their derivatives with respect to the primary variables.
This is essential for robust Newton convergence.

The current HPC version includes:

- fully implicit time stepping;
- monolithic Newton linearization;
- analytical Jacobian blocks for the two pressure variables;
- lumped transient terms for agreement with the reference solver;
- cell-centered nonlinear mobilities for controlled comparison with MATLAB;
- custom damping to protect the capillary-pressure gap during early Newton iterations;
- PETSc/MUMPS direct solves as a robust baseline before iterative preconditioning.

<img src="/assets/images/07hhnewton/hhnewton_newton_history.svg" width="720"/>

The direct solver baseline is useful because it separates nonlinear modeling questions from preconditioner design.
Once the discretization and Newton strategy are verified, the same block structure can be exploited by scalable preconditioners for large three-dimensional simulations.

## HPC relevance

The scientific motivation is not the solution of a small benchmark problem.
Realistic CO$_2$ storage simulations require large domains, heterogeneous permeability fields, long injection times, and a nonlinear front that must remain stable under aggressive time stepping.
This is exactly the regime where a verified MATLAB prototype is no longer sufficient and an HPC implementation becomes necessary.

In the MOOSE branch, mesh partitioning, sparse matrix assembly, nonlinear solves, and linear algebra are all handled in a distributed-memory environment.
This allows HHNewton to move from one-dimensional verification tests to two- and three-dimensional geometries suitable for application-driven studies.

<img src="/assets/images/07hhnewton/hhnewton_strong_scaling.svg" width="720"/>

The figure summarizes the type of strong-scaling diagnostic used during development.
The long-term target is to replace the direct factorization baseline with block preconditioners that exploit the physics:
storage-dominated reaction terms, elliptic pressure diffusion, and saturation-driven off-diagonal coupling.

## Toward hydro-mechanics

The next step is to couple the two-phase flow model to mechanics.
This extension introduces the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ and a quasi-static balance of momentum,

$$
-\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma}(\mathbf{u},p_w,p_c)
=
\mathbf{f}.
$$

In a poromechanical formulation, deformation changes porosity and permeability,

$$
n = n(\mathbf{u}),
\qquad
\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{k}(\mathbf{u}),
$$

and the fluid equations become coupled to the evolving pore volume.
In a finite-strain setting one may write the storage terms in a reference configuration through the Jacobian $J$ of the deformation,

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(J n S_w \rho_w\right)
+ \mathrm{Div}\left(\rho_w \mathbf{Q}_w\right)
= J Q_w,
$$

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(J n (1-S_w) \rho_c\right)
+ \mathrm{Div}\left(\rho_c \mathbf{Q}_c\right)
= J Q_c.
$$

This is the point where the MOOSE/HPC implementation becomes particularly important.
The same framework can host flow kernels, mechanics kernels, material models, and block preconditioners in a single multiphysics environment.
The MATLAB branch remains valuable as a reduced verification tool, while the MOOSE branch becomes the platform for realistic hydro-mechanical simulations.

## Why this matters

HHNewton is designed as a bridge between transparent numerical analysis and HPC-scale multiphysics simulation.
The MATLAB implementation provides confidence in the algebra.
The MOOSE implementation provides a path to realistic geometries, large meshes, and scalable solvers.

For proposal-level applications, this matters because the project is not limited to a single demonstrator.
It defines a workflow:

- derive and verify the nonlinear two-phase model in MATLAB;
- port the verified residual/Jacobian structure to MOOSE;
- run distributed-memory simulations for realistic CO$_2$ injection scenarios;
- extend the same framework to hydro-mechanical coupling.

This combination makes HHNewton a practical numerical laboratory for developing, validating, and scaling nonlinear porous-media models relevant to subsurface energy and environmental applications.
